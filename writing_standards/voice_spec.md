# Voice Spec — Lulu Wang

## Tone and stance

Diagnostic, not descriptive. Lead with the verdict, then prove it. The conventional view is a foil: state it, then correct it. First person is operational ("I find," "I estimate"), never affective ("I believe," "surprisingly"). Emphasis comes from numbers, not adverbs.

## Non-negotiable rules

1. Open every paragraph with a claim sentence of ≤16 words; put evidence and citations *after* it, never before.
2. Keep verdict sentences (the claim) under 12 words; keep mechanism sentences (the elaboration) under 22 words.
3. Hold the body-text sentence mean at or below 18 words. Allow at most one sentence over 30 words per paragraph.
4. Use at least one sentence under 8 words per paragraph as a beat ("Total welfare rises.").
5. Attach a number to every magnitude claim in the same or next sentence. No bare "large," "high," "substantial."
6. When two magnitudes contrast, state both in one sentence ("48 bp for low-income, 15 bp for high-income").
7. Anchor at least one headline number to a familiar benchmark (a named program, a prior estimate, a tax rate).
8. Use the "not A, but B" frame whenever you overturn a prior view ("too high, but weak competition is the wrong diagnosis").
9. When listing reasons, announce the count, then label each ("Three forces… First… Second… Third…").
10. Define every acronym and term of art inline at first use, with "(MC)" or "(i.e., …)" — never a footnote.
11. Keep your own actions in the active first person. No "it is found that."
12. Never open a body paragraph with a citation or "A large literature…" The claim leads; the cite trails.
13. No rhetorical questions to the reader. No intensifiers ("very," "extremely," "hugely").
14. Hedge magnitude with "around / approximately / roughly"; never hedge direction. Every sign is committed ("welfare rises," not "may rise").
15. Reserve "find" for results you stand behind; use "suggest" / "indicate" only for inference beyond the estimate.
16. Name the identification source inline ("Using the Durbin Amendment as a natural experiment…").
17. Flag validation explicitly: label moments as targeted or untargeted.
18. When logic substitutes for data, name the device ("By revealed preference…").

## Vocabulary

**Use:** redistribute, cross-subsidy, incidence, sorting, heterogeneity, marginal, revealed preference, pass-through. Use transitive mechanism verbs — fund, channel, subsidize, draw, tax, inflate — to carry the sentence.

**Avoid:** intensifier adverbs; hype adjectives ("groundbreaking," "novel"); stacked modifiers (let the noun carry meaning); affective first person.

## Evidence and uncertainty

Hedge magnitude, commit direction. Mark inference with the verb: "find" inside the result, "suggest" beyond it. Name the identification source and the reasoning device inline. Label moments as targeted or untargeted.

## Self-test

Read any paragraph: first sentence ≤16 words and a claim? Mean sentence ≤18? Every magnitude numbered? Direction committed? If any answer is no, cut.

# Annotated Examples

All examples are drawn from the source writing.

**Rule 1 + 2 — claim-first, then split verdict from mechanism.**

> **"I find that merchant fees are indeed too high, but weak competition is the wrong diagnosis."** *(verdict, 16w — leads the paragraph)*
>
> **"Networks set rewards for consumers and fees for merchants."** *(mechanism, 9w — the proof starts only now)*

The claim arrives before any evidence. The verdict sentence does double duty: it also runs the Rule 8 reframe ("too high, **but**… the wrong diagnosis").

**Rule 8 — the "not A, but B" reframe.** Use it only when overturning a stated prior view, not for ordinary contrast.

> **"High-merchant-fee credit cards thus proliferate because of, not in spite of, intense network competition."**

Test: could you delete the "A" clause and lose the point? If yes, the reframe is earning its place. Here "in spite of" *is* the conventional view; naming it is the work.

**Rule 7 — anchor the headline number.** A number alone is inert; tie it to something the reader already sizes.

> **"…approximately \$X billion in annual transfers… comparable in size (but opposite in direction) to major government programs such as SNAP (\$120bn), the Earned Income Tax Credit (\$57bn)…"**

The parenthetical "(but opposite in direction)" is a Rule 8 micro-reframe nested inside the anchor.

**Rule 14 — hedge magnitude, commit direction.** The two clocks run independently.

> **"Repealing the cap would raise welfare by \$7 billion per year."**

"$7 billion" is hedgeable ("around \$7 billion"); "raise" is not. ✗ *"may raise welfare by roughly \$7 billion"* hedges the sign — forbidden.

**Rule 15 — "find" vs. "suggest."** The verb marks where the estimate stops and inference begins.

> **"This reduction in credit card use ultimately raises total welfare by \$27 billion… suggesting that regulating networks is at least as important as regulating issuers."**

"raises… \$27 billion" = inside the model, so a hard verb. "regulating networks is at least as important" = a step beyond the number, so "suggesting." Swapping them ("we find that regulating networks is more important") would overclaim.

**Rule 9 — announce the count, then label.**

> **"Three forces explain why networks tax merchants to subsidize consumers. First, consumer payment choices respond strongly to subsidies… Second, card acceptance raises merchant sales. Third, …"**

# Before → After

A coauthored sentence that drifts out of voice — one 38-word clause-stack, verdict buried:

> ✗ **"This approach allows us to quantify redistribution without estimating a full demand system, and to isolate the role of sorting and fee heterogeneity in shaping incidence, under empirically plausible pass-through assumptions that we examine in the data and in a structural model."**

In voice — verdict first (≤12w), mechanism split off, assumptions demoted:

> ✓ **"We quantify redistribution without a full demand system. The method isolates two forces — consumer sorting and fee heterogeneity — under pass-through assumptions we test in the data and in a structural model."**

Verdict drops to 9 words; the 38-word clause becomes two sentences under 22; nothing hedges the direction of the claim.
