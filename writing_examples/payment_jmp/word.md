Credit card networks fund consumer rewards through merchant fees. Because merchants rarely surcharge, consumers do not internalize the costs of payments, making credit card adoption socially excessive. I develop and estimate a model of platform competition to evaluate policy responses. Capping merchant fees at the cost of cash raises welfare by \$31 billion. Because networks compete for consumers through greater rewards rather than for merchants with lower fees, competition inflates credit card use and reduces welfare by \$12 billion relative to monopoly. Dual-routing mandates increase consumer multihoming, redirect competition toward merchants, and raise welfare by \$8 billion. The direction of competition matters more than its intensity.
