### - [ ] Lines 9-10: Weak verb "speak to"

**Comment:** "The counterfactual results speak to contemporary legal and policy debates" uses the weak, colloquial phrase "speak to." In academic prose, the stronger verb is "address" or "engage with" or "illuminate."

**Original:**
```
The counterfactual results speak to contemporary legal and policy debates in payments and platform markets.
```

**Proposed Revision:**
```
The counterfactual results illuminate contemporary legal and policy debates in payments and platform markets.
```

**Why better:** "Illuminate" is more precise and direct than the colloquial "speak to." It clarifies the relationship: the results shed light on the debates rather than merely "speaking" to them.

### - [ ] Lines 23-24: Redundant framing "which is the harm that"

**Comment:** "A monopoly network raises per-transaction fees, which is the harm that antitrust cases have targeted" uses the redundant construction "which is the harm that." Simpler: "A monopoly network raises per-transaction fees — the harm antitrust cases target."

**Original:**
```
A monopoly network raises per-transaction fees, which is the harm that antitrust cases have targeted.
```

**Proposed Revision:**
```
A monopoly network raises per-transaction fees — the primary target of antitrust cases.
```

**Why better:** Removes redundant relative clause structure ("which is... that") and tightens the connection between the fee increase and its antitrust significance.

### - [ ] Lines 28-29: Weak verb "targets"

**Comment:** "Antitrust typically targets harms from low output" uses "targets" as a weak verb choice. The more direct verb is "addresses" or "focuses on."

**Original:**
```
Antitrust typically targets harms from low output.
```

**Proposed Revision:**
```
Antitrust typically addresses harms from low output.
```

**Why better:** "Addresses" is more precise — antitrust laws are designed to address (solve/remedy) harms, not just "target" (aim at) them.
