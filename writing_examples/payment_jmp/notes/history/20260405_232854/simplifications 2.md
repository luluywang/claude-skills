# Suggested Simplifications

## [response_round2.tex]

### Priority 1: Reduce hedging and defensive language (High impact on tone)

**Issue:** Lines 56–66 (opening gratitude to editor)

The letter opens with effusive praise: "I deeply appreciate your comments on how to revise the paper in order to maximize its potential." This reads as brown-nosing and is inconsistent with the author's direct, professional voice elsewhere.

**Suggested fix:** Replace with "Thank you for your comments on revision. The referees provided constructive guidance, and the paper has improved substantially."

**Benefit:** Maintains professional respect without register oscillation toward flattery. Sets a clearer tone for the substantive responses that follow.

---

**Issue:** Lines 224–230 (credit constraints response)

The author writes "I share the referees' discomfort with the no-substitution assumption" and then hedges the welfare conclusion with "However, this does not change the estimated welfare results."

**Suggested fix:** Rewrite as: "The no-substitution assumption is a limitation. The welfare results remain robust: $27 billion in the baseline versus $31 billion under credit constraints, both estimated to match the Durbin Amendment moment. The policy ranking is unchanged across all counterfactuals."

**Benefit:** Removes unnecessary softening language. States the equivalence of results directly rather than as a defensive caveat.

---

**Issue:** Lines 838–845 (fixed costs limitation)

The response begins "I agree that fixed costs of card acceptance are potentially important" and then justifies why they cannot be identified. This defensive structure can be streamlined.

**Suggested fix:** "Fixed costs of card acceptance cannot be identified separately from sales benefits without exogenous variation in consumer adoption that shifts merchant behavior. This fundamental limitation motivates the choice of counterfactual: capping fees at 120 basis points (comparable to Canadian levels) keeps the analysis within empirically observed merchant behavior rather than requiring extrapolation to unmapped equilibria."

**Benefit:** Directly acknowledges the constraint and explains its consequence without defensive framing.

### Priority 2: Cut redundant hedging and approximation language (Medium impact)

**Issue:** Lines 37–40 (Summary of main changes)

"The main fee cap is now 120 bps, roughly half the current level, which captures about 80% of the Ramsey planner's welfare gains"

Multiple hedges ("roughly," "about") in a single statement create uncertainty where the numbers are hardcoded facts.

**Suggested fix:** "The main fee cap is now 120 bps, which captures 80% of the Ramsey planner's welfare gains ($27 bn vs. $35 bn)."

**Benefit:** Cleaner, more authoritative. The numbers stand on their own.

---

**Issue:** Lines 288 (welfare comparison)

"The cost-of-cash cap delivers roughly 7% higher welfare gains than the moderate cap."

The approximation hedge weakens a precisely calculated figure in a response letter where precision is valued.

**Suggested fix:** "The cost-of-cash cap delivers 7% higher welfare gains than the moderate cap (approximately $2 billion additional)."

**Benefit:** Quantify the hedge or drop it. If you're hedging, explain what aspect is approximate.

---

**Issue:** Lines 701–708 (cash preference shocks)

Multiple hedges strung together: "would be overestimated," "would be underestimated," "partially offset," "directionally robust." While the logic is sound, the presentation reads as over-cautious.

**Suggested fix:** "A richer model would overestimate credit aversion per cardholder but underestimate the number of cardholders—these biases offset in aggregate welfare calculations, leaving the main conclusions intact."

**Benefit:** Present offsetting forces as symmetrical facts, not defensively. Tighten the explanation.

### Priority 4: Improve logical flow and argument clarity (Medium impact)

**Issue:** Lines 120–127 (Durbin evidence reframing)

The explanation announces the finding ("The Durbin Amendment cut large issuers' interchange revenue; those issuers responded...") before explaining why this matters. No narrative tension.

**Suggested fix:** Lead with the mechanism, then abstract: "The Durbin Amendment cut large issuers' interchange revenue. Those issuers responded by cutting rewards, reducing advertising, and scaling back teller incentives simultaneously—their debit volumes fell. This reveals that interchange revenue funds issuer-side promotion broadly. When issuers lose that revenue, payment volumes shift."

**Benefit:** Creates narrative flow. Readers see what happened (debit decline), then understand why (funding cuts), then abstract the principle.

---

**Issue:** Lines 892–903 (information environment and sorting)

The response conflates two ideas: (1) the information assumption and (2) why perfect sorting doesn't occur despite complete information.

**Suggested fix:** Separate them: "The model assumes consumers have complete information about merchant prices and acceptance. Given this, consumers do not sort perfectly across merchants because CES preferences value variety, not because of information gaps. Business stealing is endogenous: a merchant's sales gain from card acceptance shrinks as competing merchants also accept, since cardholders spread spending across more stores."

**Benefit:** Clearer logical structure. Each claim has its own supporting explanation.

---

**Issue:** Lines 1097–1099 (income gradient explanation)

The em-dash and conditional phrasing ("does not derive utility through the volume channel alone") obscures a straightforward point.

**Suggested fix:** "A higher-income consumer earns more rewards in dollars but not greater utility from the same reward rate percentage. The income gradient $\alpha_y$ captures a genuine preference difference: conditional on the same reward rate, higher-income consumers are more responsive to rewards in adoption decisions."

**Benefit:** Separates math (income → more dollars) from interpretation (preference heterogeneity). Removes em-dash and conditional language.
