# RAP Seed (drafted from intro.tex; user to review)

**Research question:**
> How does network competition affect merchant fees, consumer rewards, and welfare in payment markets, and which policies (fee caps, dual routing, mergers, public options) improve total welfare?

**Main answer / finding:**
> "Merchant fees are indeed too high, but weak competition is the wrong diagnosis." Networks compete intensely for cardholders by funding rewards through merchant fees; under price coherence, this generates excessive credit card adoption. Capping credit merchant fees raises total welfare by \$27 billion annually. The direction of competition matters, not just its intensity: dual-routing mandates redirect competition from rewards toward merchant fees, also raising welfare.

**Positioning:**
> Existing two-sided market theory cannot resolve whether network competition raises or lowers fees and welfare — it depends on consumer multi-homing, which the U.S. market has in mixed measure. Closest empirical predecessor is Huynh et al. (2022), which estimates a structural two-sided model of consumer and merchant card adoption; this paper builds on it by modeling merchant and network competition. Distinguishes itself from the market-power story (Cuesta & Sepúlveda 2021) by showing that the welfare problem is rewards-driven over-adoption of credit, not market power restricting output.

**Promised deliverables by section:**
- §2 Institutional Details and Data: trace the fee flow (merchants → networks → issuers → consumer rewards); describe the data sources used to identify consumer and merchant sensitivities.
- §3 Reduced-Form Facts: three facts — (i) consumer payment choices respond strongly to subsidies (Durbin debit-spend reduction), (ii) accepting cards raises merchant sales (event study), (iii) ~X% of consumers single-home credit cards.
- §4 Model: structural two-sided platform competition with single- and multi-homing consumers; merchant pricing and acceptance under price coherence; network profit maximization.
- §5 Estimation: match reduced-form facts, payment surveys, and aggregate market data; show out-of-sample predictions (e.g., AmEx OptBlue acceptance response, retail margins).
- §6 Counterfactuals: cap credit merchant fees (+\$27B), uncap debit (+\$7B), monopoly merger (+\$15B), dual-routing (positive), CBDC (positive but smaller).
- §7 Conclusion: regulating networks is at least as important as regulating issuers; the direction of network competition matters; merger antitrust intuitions don't transfer cleanly.
