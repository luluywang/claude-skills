  Text:   "Large differences in interchange fees across payment methods imply that these cross-subsidies can be substantial. However, two mechanisms substantially limit cross-subsidization in practice. First, consumer sorting—where consumers who use different payment methods shop at different merchants—limits the exposure of cash and debit users to the effects of high interchange fees. Where users of different payment methods do overlap, such as at large grocery stores, interchange fees are 50% lower due to a combination of sector discounts and private negotiations. We embed these facts in a sufficient-statistics framework to quantify the welfare effects of interchange fees across consumer groups. We estimate that interchange fees transfer approximately $30 billion every year from cash and debit users (who pay higher retail prices) to credit card users (who receive rewards). These transfers are comparable in scale but opposite in direction to major government transfer programs. Consumer sorting and merchant fee heterogeneity reduce regressivity by 25 %, but do not eliminate it. Finally, we show that both the Durbin Amendment and the recent rise of premium credit cards have been regressive, with significant implications for competition, regulation, and the distributional impact of financial innovation."
